Charged Particle Simulation for Magnetic Sail Propulsion

Research project in collaboration with Dr. Andrew J. Higgins at McGill University (January 2024 - June 2024)

Project Overview

This independent research project, conducted in collaboration with Dr. Andrew J. Higgins at McGill University, focused on simulating the motion of charged particles in magnetic fields to analyze the feasibility of magnetic sail propulsion systems for interstellar travel. Magnetic sails offer propellantless propulsion by using solar wind and charged particles to generate thrust, making them ideal for long-duration space missions.

Technical Implementation

The simulation was built using Python with NumPy for numerical computations and Matplotlib for 3D visualization. I implemented both Euler's method and the Runge-Kutta 4th order method (RK4) to solve the equations of motion. The simulation calculates the magnetic field using the Biot-Savart law and applies the Lorentz force law to determine particle trajectories in three different configurations: infinite wire, finite wire, and looped wire magnetic fields.

Key Findings

The project successfully demonstrated that charged particles follow spiral-like paths when moving through non-uniform magnetic fields, confirming theoretical predictions about magnetic sail behavior. The RK4 method provided significantly higher accuracy compared to Euler's method, especially for complex field geometries. The research validated that magnetic sails can effectively deflect solar wind particles to generate thrust, supporting their potential as efficient propulsion systems for future interstellar missions.

Research Contributions

This work provides a computational foundation for studying charged particle behavior in advanced space propulsion systems. The simulations offer insights into the practical implementation challenges of magnetic sails, including the need for large superconducting loops (up to 50 km radius) and the technical difficulties of deploying such structures in space. The project contributes to the growing body of research on propellantless propulsion systems for long-duration space travel.

Skills Developed

Through this project, I developed advanced skills in computational physics, numerical methods, and scientific programming. I gained experience with data visualization using Matplotlib, efficient data management with Pandas, and implementing complex physical models in Python. The project also enhanced my understanding of electromagnetic theory, aerospace propulsion systems, and research collaboration in an academic setting.

Solar-Powered Automated Plant Management System

Design and simulation of a next-generation propulsion system for small spacecraft

Project Overview

Solar Stack is a solar-powered, autonomous vertical plant growth system designed to address limited urban gardening space and high-maintenance irrigation solutions. The project focused on designing a compact, modular enclosure capable of automatically monitoring soil moisture and delivering precise water volumes to multiple plants without user intervention. The system emphasizes sustainability, low maintenance, and scalability for urban and residential environments.

System Design & Implementation

Solar Stack is a solar-powered, autonomous vertical irrigation system designed for urban gardening in limited spaces. The system automatically monitors soil moisture and delivers precise water volumes to multiple plants.

Prototyping & Testing

A full-scale prototype was fabricated using additive manufacturing and tested with real plants. Validation included water flow regulation, leak prevention, solar reliability, structural stability, and fully autonomous operation.

Skills & Outcomes

This project developed skills in mechanical design, rapid prototyping, system integration, and sustainable engineering. The final prototype demonstrated a low-maintenance, scalable solution for compact plant growth.
